Green, AW (1858 - 1935)

Born
1858
Died
21 August 1935

Occupation
Philanthropist, Protestant and Public Servant
Alternative Names
  • Green, Alfred William

AW Green was a New South Wales public servant for who spent his retirement working privately in child welfare. His life shows the many links between public and private agencies in New South Wales.

Details

Green began his government career in 1872 and in 1884 joined the State Children's Relief Board and later became Boarding Out Officer under Charles Mackellar. Green succeeded Mackellar as President of the State Children's Relief Board in 1914, continuing many of Mackellar's ideas. He was highly sympathetic to single mothers, and believed the state should pay destitute mothers to board their own children: during his time more than half the foster children in New South Wales were actually living with their own mothers or near relatives. He extended Mackellar's system of building residential homes for working mothers with babies. He was a strong critic of the Aborigines Protection Board from 1915-1917, claiming too many Aboriginal children ended up in Mittagong and other State Children's Relief Board homes, but later came to a working arrangement with that agency.

Green believed the boarding-out system in New South Wales was 'the best in the world.' He said child welfare work was charity, disagreeing with reformers who argued the education of state children would reduce poverty and disadvantage. Green retired in 1922, at the time the State Children's Relief Department was replaced by the Child Welfare Department.

After his retirement Green, an Anglican, joined the Aborigines Protection Board and chaired the executive committee of Dr Barnardo's Australia. He was president of the Church of England Orphanage from 1922 until 1927, then of the Church of England Children's Homes until his death. He was also involved with children's homes run by the Masons at Lewisham and Baulkham Hills, and set up Mowbray Park Home at Picton.

As well as this, Green was president of the Parks and Playgrounds Movement, assistant secretary of the British Medical Association (New South Wales) and of the Medical Union of New South Wales. He was associated with hospitals and musical societies and was intensely involved with cricket, serving 40 years on the New South Wales Cricket Association and as president of Western Suburbs Club. He also served on the committees of western Sydney bowling clubs and rugby union clubs.
