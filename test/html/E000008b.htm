Summary

Selina Murray MacDonald Sutherland was born in Scotland in 1839, and came to Melbourne in 1881. Sutherland was a key figure in child rescue activities in Victoria, and the founder of the Victorian Neglected Children's Aid Society, and Sutherland Homes for Neglected and Destitute Children.

Details

Selina Murray MacDonald Sutherland was born in Scotland in 1839, and migrated to New Zealand in 1862, where she worked as a nurse. Sutherland was sometimes known as 'New Zealand's Florence Nightingale'.

In 1881, Sutherland embarked on a voyage with a planned itinerary of Fiji, Sydney, Melbourne and back to New Zealand. The steamer was shipwrecked off the Victorian coast, and Sutherland found herself in Melbourne.

In 1888, Sutherland became Victoria's first licensed 'child rescuer', under the terms of the Neglected Children's Act 1887.

Since the early 1880s, Sutherland had been a key figure in the child rescue activities of the Scots' Church. Along with Mrs Maria Armour, she founded the Scots' Church Neglected Children's Aid Society.

As a result of a rift with the Scots' Church, Sutherland formed another organisation in 1893, the Presbyterian Society for Neglected and Destitute Children.

In late 1893, there was public disagreement between Sutherland and the General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church, leading to her resignation in November. In December 1893, Sutherland founded a new, non-denominational organisation, the Victorian Neglected Children's Aid Society.

Years before, Sutherland had been involved in a carriage accident in New Zealand which left her with a shoulder injury. In the early 1900s, Sutherland was still suffering from the effects of this injury, however she continued to work as Agent and Superintendent of the Victorian Neglected Children's Aid Society.

According to biographer Nancy Groll, by 1906 Sutherland was in great pain, 'requiring daily massage treatment and medication. She also began to suffer dizzy spells, reportedly caused by a "slight cerebral problem"'.

The Society's Committee of Management called on Sutherland to resign, or take an enforced leave of absence. When she refused, they took the step of standing her down from the Neglected Children's Aid Society.

Sutherland, with some supporters who left the Society with her, formed a new organisation called the 'Sutherland Committee', based at 68 Latrobe Street, Melbourne. Soon after, they registered a new organisation, 'Sutherland Homes for Neglected and Destitute Children'.

In 1909, Miss Auguste Meglin donated a property at Diamond Creek to Sutherland Homes. It turned out that Miss Meglin and Miss Sutherland would never see Sutherland Homes for Neglected Children at the Diamond Creek site - Meglin passed away in July 1909 and Sutherland died on 8 October 1909.

Hundreds of people attended Sutherland's funeral at the Melbourne General Cemetery. Her gravestone, funded by a public appeal, is inscribed:

For twenty-eight years an unwearied friend of Melbourne's poor, the truest helper of its fallen, and the devoted foster mother of all destitute ones, for whom she taught Victoria how to care, having rescued 3,000 waifs from its streets and slums. She hath done what she could.
